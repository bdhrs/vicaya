## What surprised me
- The Pāli Canon is far more tightly organized around a single integrated meditation system than modern practice suggests. The ānāpānasati Sutta alone maps breath mindfulness onto all four satipaṭṭhānas, seven bojjhaṅgas, and four jhānas — yet modern traditions routinely teach these as separate techniques.
- The kasina practice (ten visual spheres) is described so frequently in the canon as foundational that one might expect it to be more widely taught today, but it has largely disappeared from standard curricula outside Burmese traditions.
- "Maraṇasati" as a standalone compound does not appear verbatim in the suttas — death contemplation lives inside other frameworks (anicca-saññā, charnel-ground contemplations in kāyagatāsati, dasadhamma).
- Ānāpānasati is explicitly called "one thing" (ekadhamma) in the canon — a single practice that unfolds into the entire satipaṭṭhāna framework. This contrasts sharply with modern presentations that treat it as one technique among many.
